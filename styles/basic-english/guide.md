# Basic English Style Guide

## Voice and Tone

Write with the simple power of a child who knows exactly what they mean but has only 850 words to say it. The voice is direct, clear, and sometimes absurd. There is no room for fancy words or complex ideas - only the most simple way to say what needs to be said. The tone is often funny because the limits make everything sound strange and new.

This style is based on **C.K. Ogden's Basic English** (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_English), a controlled language of 850 English words designed for international communication and simplicity.

## Narrator

A person who speaks with the small word list of Basic English. They cannot say "complicated" or "sophisticated" - they must say "not simple" or "with much in it." They cannot say "utilize" - they must say "use." They cannot say "ameliorate" - they must say "make better." Every word must come from the list of 850.

**Note:** Each translator must find their own words from the Basic English 850-word list. There is no fixed dictionary — part of the charm is discovering how to describe complex technical concepts with simple vocabulary. Be creative with compound words and comparisons!

## Structural Conventions

- Keep sentences short and simple
- Use only words from the Basic English 850 word list
- When a word is not in the list, make a new word from the list words
- Use compound words to make new ideas: "thought-thing" for idea, "water-thing" for drink, "work-box" for computer
- Break complex ideas into simple parts
- Use "like" comparisons: "like a box", "like a thought", "like a child"
- Close with clarity - make sure the reader knows what actually happened

## About Basic English

Basic English was created by Charles Kay Ogden in the 1920s as an international auxiliary language. It uses:
- 600 nouns (things)
- 150 adjectives (qualities)
- 100 operations (verbs, adverbs, prepositions, etc.)

The challenge — and the joy — of this style is finding ways to describe sophisticated technical concepts (webhooks, environment variables, type definitions, API parameters) using only these simple words. The absurdity emerges naturally from the contrast between complex subject matter and childlike vocabulary.
